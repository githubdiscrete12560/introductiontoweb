A matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers, but its true power lies in how we interpret it. Understanding these three viewpoints is key to unlocking their utility in graphics, data science, and physics.

Core Operations

Multiplying matrices and vectors is the engine of linear algebra. These operations combine data and compose transformations, with different viewpoints offering unique insights into the process.

Matrix-Vector Multiplication

This operation transforms a vector. It can be seen as a weighted sum of the matrix's columns (Column View) or a series of dot products with the matrix's rows (Row View). Both methods yield the same result: a new, transformed vector.

Matrix-Matrix Multiplication

This operation combines two transformations into one. The element in the result's first row and first column, for example, is the dot product of the first matrix's first row and the second matrix's first column. The order matters: AB is not the same as BA.

Matrices as Geometric Transformations

In Euclidean space (our familiar grid), matrices don't just sit on a page; they actively manipulate points and shapes. Multiplying a vector by a matrix transforms it, leading to scaling, rotation, shearing, and more. Here, we see how different matrices transform a simple unit square.

Scaling

Stretches or shrinks space. Uneven scaling values will distort proportions.

Rotation

Rotates all points around the origin by a specified angle.

Shearing

Tilts one axis relative to another, turning squares into parallelograms.

The Power of Homogeneous Coordinates

Standard matrix multiplication can scale, rotate, and shear, but it can't perform translation (shifting). This is because it must always leave the origin (0,0) unchanged. To overcome this, we use homogeneous coordinates, a clever trick that adds an extra dimension.

By representing a 2D point (x, y) as a 3D point (x, y, 1), we can use a larger 3x3 matrix. This new matrix has room to encode translation, alongside rotation and scaling. It allows us to combine a whole sequence of transformations into a single matrix, which is incredibly efficient for computer graphics and robotics.

Key Takeaways

  • Matrices offer multiple perspectives: rows, columns, and transformations.
  • Matrix operations are the engine for transforming data and space.
  • Homogeneous coordinates enable translation and the composition of complex transformations.
