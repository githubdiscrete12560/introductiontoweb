An Introduction to Matrices

Understanding the Building Blocks of Linear Algebra

What is a Matrix? Three Perspectives

A matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers arranged in rows and columns. It's a fundamental tool for representing and manipulating data and transformations.

  • Row View: A stack of row vectors.
  • Column View: A collection of column vectors.
  • Basis Vector View: A description of a linear transformation.

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \end{bmatrix} $$

The Row and Column Views

Row View

Think of the matrix as a set of horizontal vectors stacked on top of each other.

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{r_1} \\ \mathbf{r_2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} [a, b, c] \\ [d, e, f] \end{bmatrix} $$

Column View

Think of the matrix as a set of vertical vectors placed side-by-side.

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{c_1} & \mathbf{c_2} & \mathbf{c_3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a \\ d \end{pmatrix} & \begin{pmatrix} b \\ e \end{pmatrix} & \begin{pmatrix} c \\ f \end{pmatrix} \end{bmatrix} $$

The Basis Vector View

A matrix can be seen as a recipe for transforming space. The columns of the matrix tell us where the basis vectors of the original space land after the transformation.

In 2D Cartesian space, the standard basis vectors are: $\hat{i} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\hat{j} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

If we have a matrix $M = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$:

  • The first column $\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ is the new position of $\hat{i}$.
  • The second column $\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$ is the new position of $\hat{j}$.

Matrix-Vector Multiplication (Column View)

Multiplying a matrix `A` by a vector `x` can be interpreted as taking a linear combination of the columns of A. The components of the vector `x` act as the weights.

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{c_1} & \mathbf{c_2} \end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix}$. Then, $A\mathbf{x} = x_1\mathbf{c_1} + x_2\mathbf{c_2}$.

Example:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} = 4 \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + 5 \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 19 \end{pmatrix} $$

Matrix-Vector Multiplication (Row View)

Alternatively, we can see multiplication as a series of dot products. Each element in the resulting vector is the dot product of a row from the matrix A and the vector `x`.

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{r_1} \\ \mathbf{r_2} \end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix}$. Then, $A\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{r_1} \cdot \mathbf{x} \\ \mathbf{r_2} \cdot \mathbf{x} \end{pmatrix}$.

Example:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} (2)(4) + (-1)(5) \\ (1)(4) + (3)(5) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 19 \end{pmatrix} $$

Matrix-Matrix Multiplication (AB = C)

Matrix multiplication represents the composition of two linear transformations. If transformation `B` is followed by `A`, the combined transformation is `C = AB`.

Rule: To multiply $A$ (m x n) by $B$ (n x p), the columns in `A` must equal the rows in `B`. The result `C` will be (m x p).

The element $C_{ij}$ is the dot product of the $i^{th}$ row of `A` and the $j^{th}$ column of `B`.

$$ C_{ij} = \mathbf{r_i^A} \cdot \mathbf{c_j^B} $$

Matrix Multiplication Example

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{bmatrix}$. Find $C = AB$.

$$ C = \begin{bmatrix} (1 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 7) & (1 \cdot 6 + 2 \cdot 8) \\ (3 \cdot 5 + 4 \cdot 7) & (3 \cdot 6 + 4 \cdot 8) \end{bmatrix} $$

$$ C = \begin{bmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{bmatrix} $$

Important: In general, $AB \neq BA$. The order matters!

Euclidean Space & Matrix Transforms

Euclidean Space ($R^n$) is the familiar space of points defined by a Cartesian coordinate system.

When we multiply a vector (a point) by a matrix, we are transforming that point to a new location.

$$ \mathbf{v}_{new} = M \mathbf{v}_{old} $$

Common transformations include: Scaling, Rotation, Shearing, and Reflection.

The Problem with Translation

A simple matrix multiplication can represent scaling, rotation, and shearing, but it cannot represent translation (shifting the origin).

This is because any linear transformation must keep the origin fixed.

$$ M \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

How do we solve this?

The Solution: Homogeneous Coordinates

We move into a higher dimension to make translation a linear transformation. For 2D space, we add a third coordinate, `w`.

  • A point $(x, y)$ becomes $(x, y, 1)$.
  • A vector (direction) $(x, y)$ becomes $(x, y, 0)$.

This allows us to use a 3x3 matrix to perform all transformations, including translation.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} x' \\ y' \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a & b & t_x \\ c & d & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Transforms in Homogeneous Coordinates

Rotation

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Scaling

$$ \begin{bmatrix} S_x & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & S_y & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Translation

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & T_x \\ 0 & 1 & T_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

The advantage is we can combine transformations (scale, rotate, translate) into a single matrix by multiplying them.

Rotation Matrix in Detail

A rotation matrix transforms points by rotating them counter-clockwise around the origin by an angle $\theta$.

It's constructed by seeing where the basis vectors land:

  • $\hat{i} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ rotates to $\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta \\ \sin\theta \end{pmatrix}$. This is the first column.
  • $\hat{j} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ rotates to $\begin{pmatrix} -\sin\theta \\ \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$. This is the second column.

$$ R(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} $$

Shear Matrix in Detail

A shear transformation slants space along one axis, leaving the other axis fixed.

For a horizontal shear with factor $k$:

  • The x-axis is fixed, so $\hat{i} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ stays the same. This is the first column.
  • The y-axis is sheared. $\hat{j} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ moves to $\begin{pmatrix} k \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$. This is the second column.

$$ S_x(k) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & k \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Summary

  • Views: Matrices can be seen as collections of row vectors, column vectors, or as a description of a transformation.
  • Matrix-Vector Multiplication: Is a linear combination of the matrix's columns or a series of dot products with its rows.
  • Matrix-Matrix Multiplication: Represents a composition of transformations. The order is crucial.
  • Euclidean Space: Matrices transform vectors (points) within this space.
  • Homogeneous Coordinates: A powerful technique to represent translation as a matrix multiplication, allowing us to combine all transformations.
