1. Introduction to Euclidean Space

üåå What is Euclidean Space?

Definition: A finite-dimensional real vector space equipped with an inner product (dot product)

üîç Key Properties

  • Distance measurement using Euclidean norm
  • Angle measurement between vectors
  • Parallel and perpendicular relationships
  • Geometric intuition matches our physical world

üî¢ Common Spaces

  • ‚Ñù¬π: Number line
  • ‚Ñù¬≤: 2D plane (computer screen)
  • ‚Ñù¬≥: 3D space (real world)
  • ‚Ñù‚Åø: Higher dimensions (data science)

Distance Formula in Euclidean Space:

$$d(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}) = \sqrt{(p_1-q_1)^2 + (p_2-q_2)^2 + \cdots + (p_n-q_n)^2}$$

üéØ Why Euclidean Space Matters for Graphics:

All computer graphics operations‚Äîfrom moving a character in a game to rendering a 3D movie‚Äîrely on Euclidean space properties. Understanding this foundation is crucial for mastering transformations!

2. Introduction to Basis Vectors in 2D

üéØ What are Basis Vectors?

Definition: A set of linearly independent vectors that span the entire vector space

Standard Basis in ‚Ñù¬≤:

$\mathbf{i} = \bmat{1 \\ 0} \quad \mathbf{j} = \bmat{0 \\ 1}$

Any vector can be written as: $\v = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}$

üîß Properties of Standard Basis

  • Orthogonal: $\mathbf{i} \cdot \mathbf{j} = 0$
  • Normalized: $|\mathbf{i}| = |\mathbf{j}| = 1$
  • Complete: Spans entire 2D space
  • Independent: No redundancy

üìä Vector Representation

$$\bmat{x \\ y} = x \bmat{1 \\ 0} + y \bmat{0 \\ 1}$$

Every point/vector is a weighted combination of basis vectors!

üí° Key Insight: Transformation = New Basis

Each column of a transformation matrix M represents what happens to the basis vectors:

$$\M = [\M\mathbf{i} \quad \M\mathbf{j}] = [\text{new } \mathbf{i} \quad \text{new } \mathbf{j}]$$

Point $(x,y)$ becomes: $x \cdot (\text{new } \mathbf{i}) + y \cdot (\text{new } \mathbf{j})$

3. Homogeneous Coordinates in 2D

üöÄ Why Homogeneous Coordinates?

Problem: Translation cannot be represented as 2√ó2 matrix multiplication!

Solution: Add an extra dimension to unify all transformations

Column Vector Convention:

$$\text{2D Point: } (x, y) \rightarrow \text{3D Homogeneous: } \bmat{x \\ y \\ 1}$$

üîÑ Coordinate Conversion

To Homogeneous: $\bmat{x \\ y} \rightarrow \bmat{x \\ y \\ 1}$
From Homogeneous: $\bmat{x \\ y \\ w} \rightarrow \bmat{x/w \\ y/w}$

‚úÖ Benefits

  • Unified operations: All transformations become matrix multiplication
  • Easy composition: Chain transformations with matrix multiplication
  • Hardware friendly: GPUs optimized for matrix operations
  • Perspective projection: Enables 3D to 2D projection

üéØ Column Vector Convention Example:

When we write transformation equations, vectors are columns and matrix multiplication is applied from the left:

$$\bmat{x' \\ y' \\ 1} = \bmat{a & b & t_x \\ c & d & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1} \bmat{x \\ y \\ 1}$$

4. 2D Rotation Matrix

üîÑ Rotation Matrix (counterclockwise by angle Œ∏):

$$\R(\th) = \bmat{\cos \th & -\sin \th & 0 \\ \sin \th & \cos \th & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$$

üîë Key Insight: Basis Vector Transformation

Each column shows where the original basis vectors go:

$\mathbf{i} = \bmat{1 \\ 0} \rightarrow \bmat{\cos \th \\ \sin \th}, \quad \mathbf{j} = \bmat{0 \\ 1} \rightarrow \bmat{-\sin \th \\ \cos \th}$

Point $(x,y)$ = weights $(x,y)$ on old basis ‚Üí same weights applied to new basis columns

üîç Example 1: Rotate point (3,1) by 90¬∞

Step 1: Set up rotation matrix for Œ∏ = 90¬∞ $$\R(90¬∞) = \bmat{\cos 90¬∞ & -\sin 90¬∞ & 0 \\ \sin 90¬∞ & \cos 90¬∞ & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1} = \bmat{0 & -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$$
Step 2: Apply transformation $$\bmat{x' \\ y' \\ 1} = \bmat{0 & -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1} \bmat{3 \\ 1 \\ 1} = \bmat{-1 \\ 3 \\ 1}$$
Step 3: Interpret result

Original point: (3,1) ‚Üí Rotated point: (-1,3)

Key insight: (3,1) was 3¬∑√Æ + 1¬∑ƒµ. After rotation: 3¬∑(new √Æ) + 1¬∑(new ƒµ) = 3¬∑(0,1) + 1¬∑(-1,0) = (-1,3)

üîç Example 2: Rotate point (1,1) by 45¬∞

Step 1: Calculate trigonometric values $$\cos 45¬∞ = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0.707, \quad \sin 45¬∞ = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0.707$$
Step 2: Set up and apply matrix $$\R(45¬∞) = \bmat{\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & 0 \\ \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$$ $$\bmat{x' \\ y' \\ 1} = \bmat{\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & 0 \\ \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1} \bmat{1 \\ 1 \\ 1} = \bmat{0 \\ \sqrt{2} \\ 1}$$
Step 3: Basis vector interpretation

New √Æ = (‚àö2/2, ‚àö2/2), New ƒµ = (-‚àö2/2, ‚àö2/2)

Result: 1¬∑(‚àö2/2, ‚àö2/2) + 1¬∑(-‚àö2/2, ‚àö2/2) = (0, ‚àö2)

üîç Example 3: Rotate triangle vertices by 30¬∞

Given: Triangle vertices A(0,0), B(2,0), C(1,2) $$\cos 30¬∞ = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 0.866, \quad \sin 30¬∞ = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$$

  • B(2,0): 2¬∑(‚àö3/2, 1/2) + 0¬∑(new ƒµ) = (‚àö3, 1)
  • C(1,2): 1¬∑(‚àö3/2, 1/2) + 2¬∑(-1/2, ‚àö3/2) = (‚àö3/2-1, 1/2+‚àö3)

Pattern: Each point keeps its original weights but applies them to the rotated basis!

5. 2D Scaling Matrix

üîç Scaling Matrix:

$$\S(s_x, s_y) = \bmat{s_x & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & s_y & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$$

üîë Key Insight: Basis Vector Scaling

Each column shows the scaled basis vectors:

$$\mathbf{i} = \bmat{1 \\ 0} \rightarrow \bmat{s_x \\ 0}, \quad \mathbf{j} = \bmat{0 \\ 1} \rightarrow \bmat{0 \\ s_y}$$

Point $(x,y)$ = weights $(x,y)$ ‚Üí same weights on scaled basis vectors

After scaling: 2¬∑$\bmat{2 \\ 0}$ + 3¬∑$\bmat{0 \\ 0.5}$ = $\bmat{4 \\ 0}$ + $\bmat{0 \\ 1.5}$ = $\bmat{4 \\ 1.5}$

Step 3: Matrix verification $$\bmat{2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1} \bmat{2 \\ 3 \\ 1} = \bmat{4 \\ 1.5 \\ 1}$$

6. 2D Shear Matrix

üîÄ Shear Matrix:

$\mathbf{H}(h_x, h_y) = \bmat{1 & h_x & 0 \\ h_y & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$

üîë Key Insight: Skewing the Basis

Shear transforms basis vectors by adding components:

$\mathbf{i} = \bmat{1 \\ 0} \rightarrow \bmat{1 \\ h_y}, \quad \mathbf{j} = \bmat{0 \\ 1} \rightarrow \bmat{h_x \\ 1}$

üîç Example 1: Horizontal shear of point (2,3) by factor 0.5

Step 1: Set up horizontal shear matrix $\mathbf{H}(0.5, 0) = \bmat{1 & 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$

New √Æ = (1,0), New ƒµ = (0.5,1)

Step 2: Apply weights to new basis

Point (2,3) = 2¬∑√Æ + 3¬∑ƒµ

Result = 2¬∑$\bmat{1 \\ 0}$ + 3¬∑$\bmat{0.5 \\ 1}$ = $\bmat{2 \\ 0}$ + $\bmat{1.5 \\ 3}$ = $\bmat{3.5 \\ 3}$

Step 3: Observation

Y-coordinate unchanged, X-coordinate shifted by: $h_x \times y = 0.5 \times 3 = 1.5$

üîç Example 2: Vertical shear creating parallelogram

Step 1: Transform unit square with vertical shear $\mathbf{H}(0, 0.8) = \bmat{1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0.8 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$

New √Æ = $\bmat{1 \\ 0.8}$, New ƒµ = $\bmat{0 \\ 1}$

Step 2: Transform vertices
  • (0,0): 0¬∑$\bmat{1 \\ 0.8}$ + 0¬∑$\bmat{0 \\ 1}$ = $\bmat{0 \\ 0}$
  • (1,0): 1¬∑$\bmat{1 \\ 0.8}$ + 0¬∑$\bmat{0 \\ 1}$ = $\bmat{1 \\ 0.8}$
  • (1,1): 1¬∑$\bmat{1 \\ 0.8}$ + 1¬∑$\bmat{0 \\ 1}$ = $\bmat{1 \\ 1.8}$
  • (0,1): 0¬∑$\bmat{1 \\ 0.8}$ + 1¬∑$\bmat{0 \\ 1}$ = $\bmat{0 \\ 1}$
Step 3: Result analysis

Square becomes parallelogram, area preserved: $\det = 1 \times 1 - 0 \times 0.8 = 1$

üîç Example 3: Combined horizontal and vertical shear

Step 1: Set up combined shear $\mathbf{H}(0.3, 0.2) = \bmat{1 & 0.3 & 0 \\ 0.2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$

New √Æ = $\bmat{1 \\ 0.2}$, New ƒµ = $\bmat{0.3 \\ 1}$

Step 2: Transform point (4,5)

Result = 4¬∑$\bmat{1 \\ 0.2}$ + 5¬∑$\bmat{0.3 \\ 1}$ = $\bmat{4 \\ 0.8}$ + $\bmat{1.5 \\ 5}$ = $\bmat{5.5 \\ 5.8}$

Step 3: Understanding the effect

Both coordinates change due to both shear components:

$x' = x + h_x \cdot y = 4 + 0.3 \times 5 = 5.5$

$y' = h_y \cdot x + y = 0.2 \times 4 + 5 = 5.8$

7. 2D Translation Matrix

üîç Translation Matrix:

$\T(t_x, t_y) = \bmat{1 & 0 & t_x \\ 0 & 1 & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$

üîë Key Insight: Homogeneous Coordinates Enable Translation

Translation adds an offset to every point while preserving the basis vectors:

$\bmat{x' \\ y' \\ 1} = \bmat{1 & 0 & t_x \\ 0 & 1 & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1} \bmat{x \\ y \\ 1} = \bmat{x + t_x \\ y + t_y \\ 1}$

  • B: (2,0) ‚Üí (2+3, 0-2) = (5,-2)
  • C: (1,3) ‚Üí (1+3, 3-2) = (4,1)

Pattern: Each point moves by the same vector (3,-2)

üîç Example 2: Center object at origin

Step 1: Object with center at (5,3)

To center at origin, translate by (-5,-3)

Step 2: Translation matrix $\T(-5,-3) = \bmat{1 & 0 & -5 \\ 0 & 1 & -3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$

üîç Example 3: Sequence of translations

First: translate by (2,1)

Step 2: Combine transformations $\T_2 \cdot \T_1 = \bmat{1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1} \bmat{1 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1} = \bmat{1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 1}$

9. Introduction to Basis Vectors in 3D

üåü Extending to Three Dimensions

3D space requires three linearly independent basis vectors

Standard Basis in ‚Ñù¬≥:

$\mathbf{i} = \bmat{1 \\ 0 \\ 0}, \quad \mathbf{j} = \bmat{0 \\ 1 \\ 0}, \quad \mathbf{k} = \bmat{0 \\ 0 \\ 1}$

Any 3D vector: $\v = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}$

üîç Right-Hand Rule

  • Thumb: +X direction
  • Fingers: +Y direction
  • Palm: faces +Z direction

This convention ensures consistent 3D coordinate systems

üéÆ Applications

  • 3D Graphics: Object positioning
  • Game Engines: Character movement
  • CAD Software: Engineering design
  • Robotics: Arm positioning

üîÑ 3D Transformation Principle

Just like 2D, each column of a 3D transformation matrix shows where the basis vectors go:

$\M = [\M\mathbf{i} \quad \M\mathbf{j} \quad \M\mathbf{k}] = [\text{new } \mathbf{i} \quad \text{new } \mathbf{j} \quad \text{new } \mathbf{k}]$

Point $(x,y,z)$ becomes: $x \cdot (\text{new } \mathbf{i}) + y \cdot (\text{new } \mathbf{j}) + z \cdot (\text{new } \mathbf{k})$
